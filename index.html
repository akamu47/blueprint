The State of Britain

Britain is not a failed country. It is a frustrated country.

Output per hour worked — the most important single measure of a nation's economic health — was only 2.4 per cent above its 2019 level at the end of 2025. Six years of work, six years of effort by millions of people, and productivity barely moved.

Had wages continued to grow at their pre-2008 trajectory, the Resolution Foundation has calculated that the average British worker would earn approximately £11,000 more per year than they do today.

The capital markets tell the same story. In 2024, eighty-eight companies delisted from the London Stock Exchange while just eighteen new companies listed. Business investment stands at 18.6 per cent of GDP — the lowest in the entire G7.

"Britain's problem is not a deficit of ideas. It is a chronic, structural failure of implementation. The British state is a graveyard of exceptional analysis."

This manifesto presents six interconnected reforms that must be treated as one machine. Remove any pillar and the programme fails. Together, they form a complete, costed, implementation-ready programme to reverse Britain's decline.

The Six Pillars of Reform

Six reforms. One machine. Each depends on the others.

Pillar 1

Institutional & Regulatory Reform

Britain's regulatory architecture has become a machine for preventing risk rather than enabling growth. The FCA alone costs £842 million per year. Financial crime compliance exceeds £38.4 billion. 603 arms-length bodies operate with minimal accountability.

£842mFCA annual cost
603Arms-length bodies

Key Proposals

  • Merge FCA and PRA into a single UK Financial Services Authority
  • Statutory growth mandate with mandatory cost-benefit analysis
  • Sunset clauses on all new financial regulation
  • Replace gold-plated AML with risk-based framework

Pillar 2

Tax Reform & Simplification

Britain's 21,000-page tax code costs £33.9 billion in annual compliance. The tax burden is set to rise to 38.5% of GDP by 2030-31. Marginal rates exceed 67% for some workers. The system punishes investment and rewards complexity.

21,000Pages of tax code
£33.9bnCompliance costs

Key Proposals

  • Merge Income Tax and National Insurance into a single levy
  • Abolish Inheritance Tax, replace with lifetime gifts framework
  • Reform Capital Gains Tax with taper relief to unlock investment
  • Replace Business Rates with land-value-based system
Read Full Chapter
Pillar 3

SME & Growth Capital

Britain's 5.5 million SMEs are starved of capital. AIM has shrunk from 1,694 to 679 companies. UK energy costs (25.33p/kWh) are nearly six times Finland's. Bank lending dominates while growth equity barely exists for scale-ups.

679AIM companies (from 1,694)
5.5mUK SMEs

Key Proposals

  • Establish UK National Investment Bank (modelled on KfW/BDC)
  • Reform Basel 3.1 SME risk weights to reduce capital cost
  • Launch Growth ISA channelling savings into productive assets
  • Create Regional Growth Funds with devolved allocation
Read Full Chapter
Pillar 4

Pensions & Savings Reform

14.6 million working-age people are undersaving for retirement. The triple lock costs £15.5 billion per year and rising. 80% of the self-employed save nothing for retirement. The UK's pension system scores poorly against international benchmarks.

14.6mPeople undersaving
£15.5bnTriple lock annual cost

Key Proposals

  • Replace triple lock with earnings-plus formula (saving £36bn by 2040)
  • Raise auto-enrolment minimum to 12% with employer matching
  • Consolidate LGPS into 6-8 super funds (Canadian model)
  • Mandatory pension saving for self-employed via HMRC

Why This Time Is Different

Britain's problem isn't diagnosis. It's delivery.

01

Implementation, Not Ideas

Britain is a graveyard of exceptional analysis. The Dilnot social care cap was legislated 13 years ago and has never had a single beneficiary. This manifesto includes the delivery architecture — not just the policy.

02

The Accountability Asymmetry

Officials are punished for visible failure but face zero consequence for invisible failure — the firm never funded, the house never built. A country that only audits downside will always underperform on upside.

03

One Machine, Not Six Policies

These six reforms cause each other. Tax simplification enables investment. Investment creates growth. Growth funds pensions. AI transforms delivery. Remove any pillar and the programme fails.

04

Self-Funding Over a Decade

AI transformation generates fiscal headroom. Triple-lock reform delivers structural savings. Dynamic revenue recovery funds transition costs. This is not a spending programme — it is an investment with measurable returns.

05

£3,500 Per Person Per Year

A sustained one percentage point increase in GDP per capita growth would deliver £3,500 more per person per year. The growth dividend transforms the fiscal arithmetic and creates headroom for further reform.

06

Reform or Managed Decline

The choice is binary. The transition costs of reform are real but finite. The cost of doing nothing is permanent — every year of stagnation is another year of wage failure, housing shortage, and inadequate pensions.
